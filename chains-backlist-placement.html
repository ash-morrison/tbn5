How Chains Are Rethinking Backlist Placement

A closer look at planograms, turns, and the economics of discovery.

Dylan CarterAugust 17, 2025New York, NY

In large chains, planograms have become the lingua franca of the sales floor. They encode the logic of discovery—how a customer’s eye lands on an endcap, glides to a table, and finally wanders the aisles. ‘We treat every linear foot as a micro‑investment,’ said Janice Lee, a national space planning lead for a top‑five retailer. ‘Turns, basket add‑ons, and visual rhythm matter as much as the titles themselves.’

Backlist curation sits at the center of that calculus. ‘The question is not whether backlist sells—it does,’ said Rafael Cortés, a veteran field rep. ‘It’s whether a title earns its keep in a specific slot this quarter.’ Cortés points to store‑level dashboards that rank titles by velocity and adjacency: craft books next to journals, cookbooks beside cookware, fantasy under a dragon‑wing endcap.

For publishers, co‑op calendars and tour routes are increasingly synchronized with resets. ‘We plan for the 30‑day window when a title can catch,’ said Imani Green, a senior sales director. ‘If it doesn’t, we reposition online and move on.’ The result: deeper but narrower in‑store backlist, with long‑tail discovery shifting to e‑commerce and libraries.

Booksellers voice mixed feelings. ‘There’s an elegance to a well‑run planogram,’ said Pauline Sanchez, a store manager in Texas. ‘But you can lose the surprises that make readers linger.’ Sanchez experiments with ‘wild cards’—one unplanned face‑out per bay chosen by staff. ‘It gives the algorithm a human heartbeat.’

Analysts say the stakes are rising as costs climb. ‘Every shelf must be defensible,’ said Marcus Flynn of RetailSight. ‘That pressures perennial series and pushes emerging authors to fight for endcap real estate where footfall is highest.’

Whether that makes stores less adventurous depends on execution. The chains that win, Lee argues, ‘tune the map but leave room for detours.’
