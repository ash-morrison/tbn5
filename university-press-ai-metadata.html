University Presses Test AI for Smarter Metadata—With Human Guardrails

Pilots focus on keywords, course tags, and discoverability, not copywriting.

Elijah CohenNovember 02, 2026Madison, WI

University presses are experimenting with machine‑assisted metadata to boost discoverability without ballooning overhead. ‘We’re not letting AI write catalog copy,’ said Dr. Hannah Boyd, marketing director at Lakeshore University Press. ‘But we are piloting tools that suggest BISAC codes, keywords, and course‑adoption tags. Humans review every suggestion.’

Early results are encouraging. ‘On backlist monographs, we’re seeing modest but real lifts in library search visibility,’ Boyd said. A pilot with three titles in political theory surfaced syllabi mentions the team hadn’t tracked before, informing outreach to specific departments.

Skeptics raise familiar concerns: bias replication, hallucinated facts, and the risk of homogenized language. ‘Guardrails matter,’ said Vikram Rao, digital strategy lead at Northwest Press. ‘We run every output through sensitivity checks and manual review. If the tool can’t explain a recommendation, we don’t use it.’

Vendors say the goal is augmentation, not automation. ‘Think spell‑check for metadata,’ said Emily Carter, product manager at the startup TagSmith. ‘Editors remain the authors of the record; the system just catches patterns and gaps across thousands of records that a person would miss.’

For small presses with skeletal marketing teams, the calculus is practical. ‘If a two‑hour pass helps a book show up in one more librarian’s search, that’s a win,’ Rao said. ‘But the final voice stays ours.’
