Inside the New Math of Sustainable Printing

Recycled stocks improve, returns decline, and smarter freight cuts emissions.

Natalie ChoNovember 06, 2026St. Paul, MN

Printers and publishers are re‑examining paper, freight, and waste in search of credible carbon reductions. ‘We’ve shifted 40% of our standard trade runs to higher‑recycled stocks,’ said Monica Reyes, COO at North Coast Printing. ‘The texture and opacity have improved dramatically in the last two years.’

Recycled inputs are only part of the picture. ‘Make‑ready waste and returns dwarf paper choices if you don’t manage them,’ said Gabe Turner, operations lead at Maple & Main. The house increased adoption of short‑run digital for testing and uses POD for backlist trickle sales. ‘Right‑sizing up front beats pulping later.’

Freight optimization is the quiet lever. Consolidating shipments and revisiting carton configurations saves fuel and reduces damages. ‘We cut one lane’s emissions by 18% with smarter routing,’ Reyes said, citing a pilot with a third‑party logistics partner.

Skepticism is healthy, sustainability managers argue. ‘We publish an annual inventory of our footprint and third‑party verify it,’ said Elijah Cohen at Riverlight Books. ‘Numbers, not vibes.’ Cohen’s team also collaborates with printers on ink choices and drying times to reduce energy usage.

For readers who care, the best signal remains durability. ‘A well‑made paperback that’s still circulating in a library ten years later is the ultimate sustainability story,’ Turner said. ‘If we build for longevity, the math gets kinder.’
