Indie Bookstores See Opportunity in Curation

Independent shops lean into staff picks, community events, and localized lists.

Sofia ReinhardtMarch 26, 2024Chicago, IL

Independent booksellers are doubling down on their superpower: curation. ‘When a regular walks in, I already have three spines in mind,’ said Gretchen Wu, co‑owner of Paper Lantern Books in Portland. ‘That intimacy is hard to scale—but it’s why people cross town to shop here.’

Programming acts as placement. Store‑run book clubs create a drumbeat for midlist novels; story times seed picture book brands; zine workshops bring teens through the door and toward the poetry shelf. ‘Events don’t just sell tickets—they shape traffic,’ said Devon Ortiz, a community manager at the seven‑store chain Harbor & Vine.

The staff pick shelf remains the beating heart. ‘Our sell‑through on staff‑stickered paperbacks is double the floor average,’ Ortiz noted. ‘Add a handwritten shelftalker and we’ve made a new classic for our neighborhood.’

Digital discovery tools supplement analog charm. Short‑form video has become a new kind of front table, with booksellers shooting 30‑second ‘if you liked X, try Y’ clips. ‘We keep it candid and local,’ Wu said. ‘Readers can tell when it’s genuine.’

Indies also collaborate on supply. Regional wholesaler partnerships let stores test small runs of niche nonfiction and comics without overcommitting. ‘Five stores ordering together can make a difference,’ said Hector Alvarez, a buyer who coordinates quarterly group buys.

As big chains optimize for speed, independents lean into serendipity. ‘We won’t beat them on breadth,’ Wu said. ‘We’ll win on taste.’
